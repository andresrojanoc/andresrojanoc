Research Interests

My research interests include atomistic simulation, fluid dynamics, electrokinetic transport, and material science. A key focus is using advanced numerical simulations to improve the fundamental description of transport phenomena in these systems. Currently, my work aims to use atomistic simulations to improve the description of the isotopic enrichment via ion irradiation of Silicon-28 layers for quantum computing architectures.

PhD Thesis

Effect of the Phenomenon of Charge Inversion over the Transport of nanoconfined electrolyte solutions

Thesis Committee

  • Dr. Shaurya Prakash (The Ohio State University).
  • Dr. Alejandro Karelovic (Universidad de Concepción).
  • Dr. José Matías Garrido (Universidad de Concepción).
  • Harvey A Zambrano (Universidad Técnica Federico Santamaría), advisor.

Abstract

Miniaturized devices integrated by nanoconduits have a great potential for applications in different technological areas due to their amplified sensibility, faster response, and increased portability. For fluids confined in nanoconduits, the enormous ratio between contact area and confined fluid volume makes the interaction between walls and fluid a non-trivial subject. Hence, transport properties deviate from classical hydrodynamics, as it is no longer suitable to predict interfacial fluid dynamics. Additionally, in nanoconduits confining aqueous electrolytes, the electrical double layer may occupy an extensive part of the conduit cross-sectional area. Since applications of nanofluidic devices usually incorporate electrolytes, a comprehensive understanding of phenomena occurring within the electrical double layer, such as charge inversion, would provide innovative solutions for relevant technological challenges. In a nanochannel, charge inversion occurs as the charge density provided by the co-ions exceeds the charge density provided by counter-ions in a specific region of the electrical double layer. In this thesis, atomistic simulations are employed to study phenomena related to the presence of charge inversion in mixtures of monovalent and multivalent ionic solutions confined in charged amorphous silica nanochannels. In particular, equilibrium and non-equilibrium molecular dynamics simulations of Poiseuille, Couette, and electroosmotic flow are conducted. Specifically, this study examines the effect of charge inversion on flow velocity distributions, slip boundary conditions, electric potential, and fluid transport properties. The results show that charge reversal of the apparent surface charge is directly related to the valence number of the counter-ions. Accordingly, a higher overscreening on the wall surface produces a high-viscosity fluid layer adjacent to the channel wall that increases with the partial concentration of multivalent counter-ions. Indeed, higher values of the interfacial viscosity and the friction coefficient associated with the presence of an excess of multivalent counter-ions near the channel wall explain interesting phenomena that take place when charge inversion occurs. Moreover, the results of this thesis will elucidate the electrokinetic effects and phenomena occurring in silicon-dioxide aqueous electrolyte interfaces. Consequently, developing more practical models and theoretical frameworks to accurately describe electrokinetic phenomena and the boundary conditions in these types of systems.

Software

A scientist by training and self-taught programmer, accustomed to working with people of different disciplines. I am interested in software development and open source science. Some of the programming languages I have worked with include Python, Fortran, Git, C++, and HTML. Furthermore, here are some highlights of my work related to computational science:

FASTTUBE: I first implemented functionalities and data extractions in the FASTTUBE molecular dynamics package at the beginning of my Ph.D. Additionally, I have mainly employed this software to assist with simulations and post-processing to study water droplets inside carbon nanotubes and over graphene sheets.
LAMMPS: I have implemented functionalities to compute a type of Coulombic interactions using a smooth truncation. Morover, I have implemented functionalities to reformulate the traditional 2T-MD scheme to overcome the limitations on the treatment of the high-energy particles by addressing the spurious double-interaction of high-energy atoms with electrons.
DLPOLY: I have implemented functionalities to reformulate the traditional 2T-MD scheme to overcome the limitations on the treatment of the high-energy particles by addressing the spurious double-interaction of high-energy atoms with electrons.
Python: I have developed scripts for manipulating LAMMPS (and other software) data files and importing them with Python. Furthermore, I am proficient in data analysis and automation of processes and have the fundamentals of deep learning.

Some of the software I write is available in my Github and Gitlab profile. Everything there is open source and external contributions are encouraged.
